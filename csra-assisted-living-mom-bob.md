Your assessment contains a critical misunderstanding regarding how Medicaid treats assets for married couples. While Medicaid does have strict asset limits, the "community spouse" (your mother) is allowed to keep a significant portion of the couple's assets, not just $2,000.

Medicaid Spend-Down Rules for Married Couples
Medicaid distinguishes between the "institutionalized spouse" (your stepfather) and the "community spouse" (your mother). Under federal guidelines, specifically the Medicaid Spousal Impoverishment Rules, your mother is not required to spend down their assets to below $2,000. 

Instead, she is entitled to keep the Community Spouse Resource Allowance (CSRA). For 2026, the federal minimum CSRA is roughly $30,828, and the maximum is approximately $154,140. Since your parents have $115,000 in assets, it is highly likely that your mother can keep all or nearly all of these assets, depending on your specific state's rules. The $2,000 limit generally applies only to the spouse entering the nursing home or memory care facility, not the one remaining at home.

Asset Protection Strategies
Even if your state's CSRA maximum is lower than $115,000, there are legal strategies to protect assets beyond the simple spend-down myth. 

Asset Allocation: An elder law attorney can help allocate assets so that your mother retains the maximum allowable amount under the CSRA.
Home Exemption: If they own a home, it is often an exempt asset as long as your mother continues to live there, regardless of its value (within equity limits).
Income Allowance: Your mother may also be entitled to a Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance (MMMNA), which allows her to keep enough of your stepfather's income (like Social Security or pension) to support herself if her own income is insufficient. 
Attempting to give away assets to family members to avoid spend-down can trigger a 5-year look-back period, resulting in a penalty period where Medicaid will not pay for care.  Therefore, professional planning is essential before transferring any money.

Immediate Steps to Take
Given your stepfather's age (88.5) and severe dementia, and your mother's inability to continue caregiving, immediate action is required to ensure his safety and her financial security. 

Secure Power of Attorney (POA): If your mother is still competent, she must immediately sign a Durable Power of Attorney for finances and healthcare to you or another trusted individual.  If she is no longer competent due to stress or her own decline, you may need to petition for guardianship or conservatorship through the court. 
Professional Assessment: Contact your local Area Agency on Aging or a geriatric care manager to assess your stepfather's needs. This documentation is crucial for Medicaid applications and determining the appropriate level of care (Assisted Living vs. Skilled Nursing/Memory Care).
Consult an Elder Law Attorney: Do not rely on general advice for asset protection. Laws vary significantly by state. An attorney can help structure the spend-down legally to maximize your mother's resources.
